Line Operations Study Guide

Takt, cycle, process, lead, and manufacturing lead time all measure duration — but on the plant floor each one answers a different question: the customer, the line supervisor, and the plant manager. Confuse them, and either the dealer lot goes empty or the yard fills with unsold inventory.

Simulation & Flow Dynamics

By adjusting available shift time and dealer orders, you set the line rate (Takt Time). If any station's Cycle Time exceeds the Takt Time, that station is a bottleneck, causing queue build-up. Follow one vehicle (Journey Timeline) to see the accumulation of logistics, active processing, and waste queues.

Time Nesting Hierarchy

Process time, manufacturing lead time, and lead time aren't separate durations — they nest inside one another, each one widening the view to include more waiting, from the weld shop out to the customer's driveway.

The Five Definitions, Plainly

Takt Time
The customer-driven heartbeat of the line. The maximum time allowed to build one vehicle and still meet dealer demand.
Cycle Time
The rate at which a single station actually produces one unit, start to finish.
Process Time
Actual work time spent on a vehicle, excluding transport and buffer queues.
Lead Time
The full elapsed period from order placement to vehicle delivery at the dealership.
Manufacturing Lead Time
The subset of lead time spent inside the plant (raw sheet metal to finished vehicle).

What is Operations?

Operations is the set of activities that transform inputs (land, labor, capital, equipment) into outputs (goods and services) that add value for customers.

Why Keep Inventory?

Holding inventories helps companies insulate operations from variations in the supply chain.

Supply Chain Flow

Reasons to Hold Inventory

  • Meet demand variation — Finished goods inventory guards against unpredictable customer demand spikes.
  • Safeguard supply variation — Raw material inventory provides a buffer against supplier delays or shortages.
  • Economic purchase order size — Buying in bulk reduces per-unit cost; raw material inventory absorbs the larger lot.
  • Process independence — WIP inventory decouples workstations so a stoppage at one stage does not immediately halt the next.

Financial Implications of Inventory

Inventory management involves managing the economic balance — maximising savings and minimising costs.

💰 Inventory Savings

Shortage Cost Avoidance

Unavailability of raw material or final product leads to lost business opportunity.

Bulk Purchase Discounts

Discounts obtained while ordering raw materials in bulk reduce unit cost.

Reduced Ordering Costs

More inventory = fewer orders placed = lower procurement & logistics cost per unit.

⚠️ Inventory Costs

Holding / Carrying Costs

All expenses related to storing and maintaining inventory in the system.

Inventory Storage Cost

Cost of building/leasing warehouses, material handling equipment, HR, utilities, etc.

Opportunity Cost of Capital

Inventory = money sitting on forklifts, shelves, and trucks in transit. That capital cannot earn returns elsewhere.

Inventory Control Systems

Two primary systems govern when and how much to order — one event-triggered, one time-triggered.

Continuous Review System

q System — Event Triggered
  • How often: System continuously tracks inventory level of each item
  • When to order: Once inventory depletes to the Re-Order Point (ROP)
  • How much: Fixed Order Quantity — typically the EOQ

Periodic Review System

p System — Time Triggered
  • How often: Inventory tracked at fixed intervals (e.g., weekly)
  • When to order: At every review period — Fixed Time Period model
  • How much: Order quantity based on predetermined Order Up Level (OUL) minus current stock
5

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)

EOQ is the ideal order quantity a company should purchase to minimise total inventory costs — ordering, receiving, and holding.

EOQ Formula
EOQ = √( 2 × D × S / H )
D = Annual Demand S = Cost per Order H = Annual Holding Cost per Unit

Key Points

  • EOQ minimises total costs of ordering, receiving, and holding inventory.
  • Best applied where demand, ordering costs, and holding costs remain constant over time.
  • Limitation: assumes demand for the company's products is constant — not realistic for seasonal or trend-driven goods.

Toyota Production System (TPS)

Produce the required output with the least possible resources — while maintaining quality and productivity. Built on two philosophies: Respect for People and Lean Manufacturing.

The TPS House

Respect for People

  • Permanent employment / job security & loyalty — workers are not treated as variable costs
  • Regular performance-linked bonuses tied to company success
  • Better supplier relations built on trust — vendors feel part of the Toyota family

Lean Manufacturing

  • Systematic elimination of all waste (Muda) from every process
  • Continuous improvement (Kaizen) driven by every employee
  • Pull production — make only what is needed, when it is needed

Just In Time (JIT)

Supplying goods as close as possible to when they are actually needed — reducing inventory and freeing up capital.

How JIT Works

  • Manufacturing: Parts and raw materials arrive just before they are added to the final product
  • Resellers: Goods arrive just before customer purchase — minimal stock on shelves
  • Benefit: Less inventory frees up cash for other investments
  • Kanban: Scheduling/signal system between production points (Pull system) — supports streamlining, efficiency, and quality

Success Factors

  • Steady, predictable production schedule
  • High-quality workmanship to avoid rework / scrap
  • No unplanned machine breakdowns
  • Reliable, geographically close suppliers

✅ Advantages

  • Short production runs — easy product switching and customisation
  • Reduced warehouse costs and space requirements
  • Less capital tied up in raw material inventory

⚠️ Disadvantages

  • High supply chain disruption risk — single supplier failure halts production
  • Example: Toyota's 1997 near-halt when a supplier fire stopped brake part deliveries
  • Sudden demand spikes may not be met without buffer stock

Lean Manufacturing

The core idea: eliminate all waste from the system so that every step adds value to the customer.

The 8 Wastes (DOWNTIME)

Products not meeting specs; requires rework or scrap

O — Overproduction

Making more than needed, or earlier than needed

W — Waiting

Idle time when people or machines wait for the next step

N — Non-Used Talent

Underutilising employee skills, creativity, and knowledge

T — Transportation

Unnecessary movement of materials, parts, or products

I — Inventory

Excess raw material, WIP, or finished goods tying up capital

M — Motion

Unnecessary movement of people within a process

E — Extra-Processing

Doing more work than the customer requires or values

The Bullwhip Effect

Increasing variability of orders (demand distortion) travelling upstream in the supply chain. Small ripples at the customer end become huge waves at the supplier end.

Demand Amplification Across the Supply Chain

Root Causes

  • Demand forecast updating — each tier adjusts forecasts, amplifying errors
  • Order batching — periodic/push ordering creates lumpy demand
  • Price fluctuations — forward buying and stocking up on discounts
  • Shortage gaming — exaggerated or duplicate orders during scarcity
  • Information gaps — poor communication across supply chain tiers

Mitigation Strategies

  • Information sharing — share real demand data (POS) across all tiers
  • Channel alignment — vendor-managed inventory (VMI), collaborative forecasting
  • Operational efficiency — reduce lead times and order minimums; move to pull systems

Project Management

"A project is a temporary endeavour undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result." — PMI. Project management = applying knowledge, skills, tools & techniques to meet project requirements.

Defining Characteristics

  • Temporary: Defined beginning and end, defined scope and resources
  • Unique: Not a routine operation — specific activities for a singular goal
  • Examples: Software development for a business process; construction of a bridge; launching a new product

The Golden Triangle

Each corner trades off against the others — e.g., compressing schedule adds pressure on scope, quality, and cost.

Interview Questions & Preparation

Operations, Supply Chain, and Logistics interviews are largely behavioural and situational. Prepare each story with the STAR framework (Situation, Task, Action, Result).

About Yourself / Why Operations?

  • Demonstrate profile fit — connect your background to the domain (work-ex, internships, projects)
  • Highlight transferable skills: problem solving, communication, stakeholder management, delegation, resilience, agility, handling pressure, seeing the bigger picture

Why This Company?

  • Research the company and role deeply before the interview
  • Speak about reputation, industry prospects, personal priorities, and job satisfaction
  • Reference recent company news, initiatives, or activities to demonstrate genuine interest
  • Express whether you prefer a dynamic start-up vs stable corporation and why it fits

Behavioural / Situational Questions

Common Themes
  • Conflict resolution with a peer or client
  • Disagreeing with your manager — how did you handle it?
  • Biggest achievement and proudest moment
  • A failure or setback — what did you learn?
  • Managing competing priorities under time pressure

Guestimates & Cases

Sometimes tied to prior experience or field of study. Structure your approach, state assumptions clearly, and walk the interviewer through your reasoning. Prepare thoroughly from the NPAD before your interview.

General Tips

Walk in prepared, authentic, and composed. The interview is as much about how you think as what you know.

Before the Interview

  • Read about the company, JD, and the specific role in detail
  • Prepare at least one thoughtful question to ask the interviewer at the end
  • Know your resume cold — be ready to expand on every bullet point

During the Interview

  • Stay positive and confident — be yourself; authenticity matters
  • Stay calm and composed — take a breath before answering complex questions
  • No "beating around the bush" — be direct and transparent; if you don't know something, say so
  • Be assertive, never aggressive
